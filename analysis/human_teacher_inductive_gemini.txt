Based on an inductive analysis of the 60 classroom exchanges, here are the emergent themes and patterns regarding how these teachers interact with their students. 

### SECTION A: EMERGENT THEMES IN TEACHER DISCOURSE

**1. The Teacher as "Amplifier" and "Translator"**
Teachers rarely accept a correct answer and simply move on. Instead, they use student utterances as raw material to broadcast a broader concept to the whole room, often translating a simple number or word into a formal definition or mathematical model.
*   *Turn 31:* A student says "Eight." The teacher amplifies: "So we would use this. We would make groups of eight... we would end up having four groups of them."
*   *Turn 56:* A student says "Three sixths." The teacher translates this into academic vocabulary: "To an equivalent fractions... This is called common denominator. He is using the common denominator."
*   *Turn 36:* A student gives a complex fraction ("15-twentieths... three-fourths"). The teacher translates this to percentages and real-world concepts: "Three-fourths is almost all of the pie... I think of three-fourths, too, is 75 percent."

**2. The Relentless Push for Justification and Process**
Teachers consistently respond to statements of fact by demanding the underlying mechanics. They want the "how" and "why" made public.
*   *Turn 18:* Student provides the correct answer and equation ("The X should be 60..."). The teacher immediately probes: "Where did you get 60 from?"
*   *Turn 54:* Student correctly lists fact families. The teacher asks them to draw it, then asks: "Can you tell us why did you put 30 on the top...?"
*   *Turn 24:* Student states a rule ("You have to make a pattern..."). Teacher prompts: "A bigger one, how?"

**3. Metacognitive Modeling (Thinking Aloud)**
Teachers frequently respond to students by narrating the internal monologue of a mathematician, modeling how a student *should* be thinking.
*   *Turn 13:* The teacher narrates her physical and mental process: "I look and I say, 'Wow. When I do that, that is less than 90 degrees...'"
*   *Turn 17:* The teacher explicitly states, "Mathematicians are thinkers all the time," and models her internal logic: "See when I write my X, I kind of make it my little cursive X so you don't mistake it for times..."

**4. Vetoing Valid Math for Pedagogical Utility**
Teachers sometimes reject mathematically correct student propositions because they do not serve the immediate pedagogical goal.
*   *Turn 34:* A student asks, "Can we still do the half?" The teacher rejects this valid fraction: "No, do something else... Do something different because it will work with any fraction."
*   *Turn 53:* A student correctly provides an equation ("5 plus 5 equals 10"). The teacher rejects it because she wants a *definition*, not an example: "You're giving me an example. I don't want an example right now."

### SECTION B: RESPONSE PATTERNS BY STUDENT UTTERANCE TYPE

**Short Utterances (1-2 words): The Teacher Does the Heavy Lifting**
Counter-intuitively, the shortest student answers often trigger the longest teacher responses. A 1-2 word answer acts as a "green light" for the teacher to launch into a lecture, an expansion, or a multi-step instruction. 
*   *Turn 2:* S says "Circumference." The teacher responds with a massive ~300-word monologue defining perimeter, circumference, formulas, and giving drawing instructions.
*   *Turn 17:* S asks a brief question. The teacher responds with a highly detailed, multi-paragraph explanation of algebraic thinking.

**Medium Utterances (3-9 words): The Pivot to Peers or Process**
When students offer a phrase or a complete thought, teachers frequently use it to pivot to the rest of the class or prompt for the next step.
*   *Turn 6:* S defines a diameter. Teacher pivots to class: "Do you agree with her on that?"
*   *Turn 15:* S says "It has to be a closed figure." Teacher validates and pivots: "What is the second rule... Student S?"

**Long Utterances (10+ words): Teacher Truncation and Redirection**
When students speak at length, teachers often give surprisingly short responses. They use the end of a long student turn to close a sequence, manage behavior, or transition to a new activity.
*   *Turn 29:* S gives a 16-word explanation of their confusion ("I know how to do it, but I don't know how to explain it"). The teacher completely ignores the content and redirects: "All right, time is up. Now find your elbow."
*   *Turn 30:* S recites 21 words of multiplication facts. The teacher responds with a management request ("Can you put the lid on that marker..."), validates, and starts a whole new historical context ("In second grade...").

### SECTION C: CLASSROOM MANAGEMENT MOVES

**1. Seamless Interleaving of Math and Management**
Teachers do not stop teaching to manage; they weave behavioral directives into the middle of mathematical sentences.
*   *Turn 12:* "Even without the traditional way, what could you do with this number right here? Give me one second please. Just one second. Excuse me. You have 92 times 8."
*   *Turn 30:* "Okay. Can you put the lid on that marker for me? Student K... you did a great job."

**2. Policing Peer-to-Peer Interaction**
Teachers actively manage *how* students speak to one another, enforcing collaborative norms over mere task completion.
*   *Turn 39:* "No, you tell him. The product is 80. You're supposed to be telling Student C this."
*   *Turn 40:* The teacher critiques the class's interaction style: "Not even talking to each other. Not saying anything out loud. You're supposed to be using your partner..."

**3. Tactical Ignoring of Off-Task Talk and Complaints**
When students attempt to derail the lesson with complaints or off-topic remarks, teachers use tactical deflection rather than engaging with the content.
*   *Turn 41:* S complains, "That's not my name... I can't pronounce it." Teacher ignores the interpersonal friction: "What should we be doing right now?"
*   *Turn 48:* S asks about a volcano in a flowerpot. Teacher gives a non-committal deflection to kill the tangent: "That's a good idea, isn't it?"
*   *Turn 47:* S complains they can't find their pencil. Teacher ignores them and threatens a different student with study hall.

### SECTION D: VARIATION ACROSS TEACHERS / TRANSCRIPTS

*   **OBSID 173 (The Lecturer):** Highly didactic. Uses student answers as springboards for long, vocabulary-dense monologues (Turns 2, 5). Focuses on formulas and compliance.
*   **OBSID 907 (The Sense-Maker):** Focuses heavily on mathematical logic and algebraic thinking. Frequently uses the phrase "Does that make sense?" (Turns 14, 16) and explicitly talks about how "mathematicians" think (Turn 17).
*   **OBSID 2196 (The Collaborator-Enforcer):** Highly focused on the social mechanics of learning. This teacher spends significant discourse time enforcing *how* partners talk to each other (Turns 39, 40).
*   **OBSID 2221 (The Taskmaster):** Discourse is heavily dominated by procedural checklists, transitions, and conditional statements ("if you are finished..."). The math talk is fragmented by intense behavioral management (Turns 43, 46, 47).
*   **OBSID 4635 (The Concept Builder):** Focuses on visual models (arrays/rectangles) and pushing students to the edge of their understanding, narrating novel student strategies to the class (Turns 57, 58, 60).

### SECTION E: WHAT IS SURPRISING

**1. The "Callousness" of Momentum**
Conventional teaching wisdom suggests validating student vulnerability. Yet, when a student admits, "I know how to do it, but I don't know how to explain it" (Turn 29), the teacher brutally cuts them off: "All right, time is up." The drive to maintain pacing and momentum often supersedes individual emotional validation.

**2. The Rejection of "Phone a Friend" as a Lifeline**
In Turn 26, the teacher says "Okay, you want to phone a friend? Okay." While framed as help, this is actually a public acknowledgement that the student has failed to answer, shifting the cognitive load away rather than scaffolding the student to the right answer. 

**3. Direct Honesty About Teacher Planning**
In Turn 23, a student asks about a test. Instead of a rigid, authoritative answer, the teacher pulls back the curtain on the co-teaching process: "No, Ms. S, and I were talking... I told her I didn't. She wants to give it Friday. I don't know... do you all think you guys are ready...?" This level of democratic vulnerability regarding lesson planning is highly unusual.

### SECTION F: IMPLICATIONS

This sample reveals that authentic classroom teaching is fundamentally different from one-on-one tutoring or idealized textbook pedagogy. 

First, **the Initiate-Respond-Evaluate (IRE) model is inadequate** to describe this discourse. Teachers rarely just "Evaluate." They *Initiate-Respond-Expand*, *Initiate-Respond-Translate*, or *Initiate-Respond-Redirect*.

Second, **cognitive load for teachers is multi-dimensional**. A teacher is never just evaluating the mathematical correctness of an answer. As seen in Turn 34 (rejecting "one half"), the teacher is evaluating the answer's *pedagogical utility* for future generalization. As seen in Turn 12, they are evaluating math while scanning the room for behavioral compliance. 

Finally, training materials often focus on how to ask good questions, but this data shows that **expert teaching relies heavily on what the teacher does *after* the student answers**. The art of teaching here lies in amplification, translation, and using one student's localized thought to advance the collective understanding of the room.
