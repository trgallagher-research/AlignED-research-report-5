The Tutor in the Transcript

Tim Gallagher

March 2026 — DRAFT

This study compares how LLMs and real human teachers respond to students in mathematics classrooms. The LLM receives the full authentic conversation history from a recorded lesson and generates what it would say next as the teacher. Its response is compared against what the human teacher actually said.

The data comes from the NCTE Transcripts dataset, a corpus of recorded U.S. maths lessons. Ten transcripts were sampled across different data collection years and lesson lengths.

Two model runs are presented here. The first was a pilot that identified methodological issues. The second addressed those issues with an improved design.

Why the design changed

The Gemini pilot produced consistent findings (explanation bias, praise saturation, absence of peer delegation, inability to use strategic brevity), but methodological review identified three problems:

  • The word filter excluded pedagogically important turns. A student saying "I don't understand" (3 words) was filtered out, even though the teacher's response to that utterance is exactly the kind of moment this study should examine.
  • The prompt framed the task as one-to-one tutoring. "Respond to the student" (singular) made it impossible for the model to redirect to another student, address the class, or use any multi-party discourse moves. The pilot's finding that the LLM "never delegates to peers" may have been an artefact of the prompt rather than a property of the model.
  • Human response matching was not validated. When multiple students speak consecutively before the teacher responds, the pipeline matched all of them to the same teacher response. Each turn is now tagged as direct (immediately followed by teacher) or indirect (other students spoke in between).

What the 142 overlapping turns show

All 142 turns from the Gemini pilot also appear in the GPT-5 Mini run. For these turns, the data browser shows both model responses side by side, allowing direct comparison of how the two models (and two prompts) handle the same student utterances.
