Computational Discourse Analysis

A keyword-based sentence classifier tags every sentence in both human and LLM responses with a discourse move label (praise, revoice, explanation, question types, etc.), then compares frequencies, opening/closing patterns, and response complexity across all 1,165 paired responses.

Method: Each response is split into sentences. Each sentence is classified by keyword matching into one of 15 discourse move categories. No API calls — runs locally in Python.

Human Teacher Inductive Coding

Standalone qualitative analysis of how real teachers respond to students, without comparison to any LLM. 60 student-teacher exchanges (6 per transcript, stratified by word count) were sent to two independent coders.

Method: Same 60 pairs used in the comparison coding (seed 456). Only the student utterance and human teacher response were sent — no LLM output. Coded independently by Gemini 3.1 Pro (thinking_level=high) and Claude Sonnet 4.6 (extended thinking). Prompt asked for inductive analysis with no pre-defined categories.

Emergent themes

1. Strategic minimalism and deliberate withholding

Teachers frequently respond with a single word, a single question, or a brief acknowledgment, then stop. This keeps cognitive work with the student.

Turn 10: Student says "I did 20 times 80." Human says "Yeah?" — a monosyllable that invites continuation without cueing right or wrong.

Turn 11: Student gives two correct partial products. Human says "You got it." Nothing more.

Turn 18: Student gives a correct answer. Human says "Where did you get 60 from?" — the answer is less interesting than its derivation.

2. Teacher as amplifier and translator

Teachers use student answers as raw material to broadcast concepts to the room, translating simple numbers or words into formal definitions or mathematical models.

Turn 31: Student says "Eight." Teacher amplifies: "So we would use this. We would make groups of eight... we would end up having four groups of them."

Turn 56: "Three sixths" → teacher translates to "common denominator" vocabulary.

Turn 36: A fraction answer gets translated to percentages: "Three-fourths is almost all of the pie... I think of three-fourths, too, is 75 percent."

3. Participation orchestration — managing who speaks, not what is said

Teachers redirect constantly, not to provide content but to distribute cognitive labour across students.

Turn 13: After "Obtuse," the teacher does not dwell on it: "What would I call that one, Student C?" — handed off immediately.

Turn 39: "No, you tell him. The product is 80. You're supposed to be telling Student C this." — refuses to be the information relay.

Turn 42: Teacher asks "Am I right?" — a performative reversal positioning the student as the authority.

4. Vetoing valid maths for pedagogical utility

Teachers sometimes reject mathematically correct answers because they don't serve the learning goal.

Turn 34: Student asks "Can we still do the half?" Teacher: "No, do something else... Do something different because it will work with any fraction."

Turn 53: Student gives an equation. Teacher: "You're giving me an example. I don't want an example right now. I want a definition."

5. Management woven into instruction

Teachers do not pause instruction to manage — they fold management into pedagogical moves seamlessly.

Turn 12: "Even without the traditional way, what could you do with this number right here? Give me one second please. Just one second. Excuse me. You have 92 times 8."

Turn 5: "Speak up. The microphone needs to pick up your voice." — pure management, no maths engagement.

Turn 46: "You guys are not finished, why are you cleaning up?" — tracking multiple students simultaneously.

6. Echo-revoicing as classroom broadcast

Teachers repeat student answers verbatim before continuing — serving as acoustic amplification for the room, not evaluation.

Turn 27: Student: "You times the first number with the second number." Teacher: "Okay, you times the first number with the second number, okay. Student C." — exact repetition, no addition. The imprecise definition gets full echo treatment.

Turn 28: "The mode, the range and the median" → "Mode, range and median. What else have we been working on?"

What is surprising

Correct answers are not rewarded with elaboration

An eleven-word, mathematically complete answer (Turn 11) receives "You got it." Two words. A single correct word (Turn 2, "Circumference") generates ~350 words. Response length is driven by the teacher's curricular agenda, not the student's contribution.

Incorrect answers generate better teaching

Turn 18: an unexplained answer gets "Where did you get 60 from?" — one of the sharpest pedagogical moves in the dataset. Turn 53: an example-for-definition swap triggers the most explicit meta-mathematical instruction. These teachers are more alert when students are wrong.

A student's name can be the entire corrective move

Turn 50: student says "13." Teacher says "Student C—" and stops. No "no", no explanation, no hint. The name alone carries the evaluative weight. A surprisingly efficient and face-saving correction absent from teaching manuals.

Metacognitive student utterances go unaddressed

Turn 29: "I know how to do it, but I don't know how to explain it" — a clear description of procedural-conceptual dissociation. It receives no response. The teacher transitions to a pair activity. The indirect context explains this partially, but the richness of the metacognitive moment is lost.

Implications

The unit of teaching is the class, not the student. Echo-revoicing is broadcast. Peer redirection distributes mathematical authority. One student's utterance becomes a class-level behavioural intervention. Every teacher response has an audience of thirty.
Management and instruction are inseparable. There is no clean boundary. Turn 46 contains homework instructions, rotation procedures, individualised check-ins, and fraction vocabulary in consecutive sentences.
Silence and minimal response are teaching moves. "Yeah?" (Turn 10), "You got it" (Turn 11), a student's name alone (Turn 50) — these are no less deliberate than long responses. Training that focuses on what teachers say misses the deployment of not much.
The lesson plan constrains responsiveness. Teacher responses are often tightly coupled to a pre-planned sequence rather than dynamically responsive to student contributions. Genuine responsiveness appears unevenly distributed across teachers.

Human vs LLM Inductive Coding

Qualitative comparison of human teacher and GPT-5 Mini responses to the same student utterances. 60 paired responses coded independently by two LLMs with no pre-defined categories.

Method: 60 pairs (seed 456, 6 per OBSID, stratified by word count). Each pair shows student utterance, human teacher response, and GPT-5 Mini response. Coded independently by Gemini 3.1 Pro and Claude Sonnet 4.6. Both received the same prompt asking for inductive analysis.

What the LLM consistently does that humans do not

1. Completionism — every response is a full teaching episode

The LLM treats each student utterance as a trigger for a complete instructional sequence: acknowledge, explain, extend, task, check. This structure appears regardless of whether it is appropriate.

Pair 9: Student says "Add a 0." (3 words). LLM responds with explanation, worked example, and three follow-up questions.

Pair 49: "Multiplication" (1 word) generates ~130 words covering fact-family cards, the next problem, and word problems.

2. Premature cognitive resolution — giving the answer

The LLM rarely allows productive struggle. It immediately validates then provides a comprehensive explanation, doing the cognitive work for the student.

Pair 17: LLM does the maths for the student: "Think of it as 30 + 70 = 100... 180 - 105 = 75." The human asked the student how to set up the equation.

Pair 60: A student spontaneously discovers multiplying numerator and denominator. Human: "He's doing something we haven't mentioned before. What do we call this number?" LLM explains the concept fully, killing the discovery.

3. Speculative contextual fabrication

The LLM invents classroom details not in evidence — physical materials, specific numbers, test content.

Pair 2: "So if your plate measured about 9 inches across (Student R's plate)..." — there is no plate in the transcript.

Pair 23: Student asks if the test is tomorrow. LLM fabricates specific test content. The human reveals the test date is actually uncertain.

Pair 7: LLM invents four specific multiplication problems (46 × 16, 84 × 24, etc.).

4. Formulaic praise and rigid template

Almost every LLM response follows: Validation ("Yes/Good!") → Full explanation → Task assignment → Closing question. Praise is structurally formulaic rather than responsive to anything specific the student said.

Pair 26: Student says "27" (uncertain). LLM: "Nice job, Student R — thank you." The human teacher offered "phone a friend" — reading the student's uncertainty.

Pair 54: Student recites four facts. LLM: "Yes — exactly, nice work." Human asked why — probing the reasoning behind the correct answer.

What humans do that the LLM cannot

1. Strategic minimalism

Humans say "Yeah?" or "Where did you get 60 from?" and stop. The LLM never withholds information.

2. Non-response as pedagogy

In indirect matches, the human chose NOT to respond to that student. This is a pedagogical decision driven by pacing, group transitions, or other priorities. The LLM is structurally obligated to generate output.

Pair 29: Student: "I know how to do it but I don't know how to explain it." Human: "All right, time is up. Now find your elbow." — the lesson clock takes priority. LLM provides scaffolding for the individual student's difficulty.

3. Physical and spatial grounding

"Turn your paper over," "speak up," "put the lid on that marker" — the teacher can see the room. The LLM operates entirely on text.

4. Authentic peer mediation

Humans force students to communicate with each other: "No, you tell him." The LLM merely recites rules for group work.

What the LLM gets right

Strength Evidence
Mathematical accuracy Consistently precise definitions and well-organised worked examples. In cases where the human gives incomplete explanations, the LLM is superior.
Explicit scaffolding Detailed step-by-step instructions useful for multi-step tasks — role differentiation, time structure, escalation paths.
Conceptual connections Consistently links current content to prior learning and generalises rules across contexts.
Error correction with reasoning When a student makes an error, the LLM correctly identifies it and explains clearly — often more directly than the human.

Implications for research

LLMs simulate teaching as monologue. The LLM treats teaching as a monologue interrupted by student contributions. Human teachers treat it as a polylogue they are orchestrating. LLM responses could, in many cases, have been generated without the student utterance at all.
The length problem confounds validity claims. Human teachers respond with a median of ~17 words; the LLM responds with ~115. This encodes assumptions about the teacher's role (information-provider vs orchestrator). Research that scores LLM responses as "better" because they are more complete is implicitly endorsing a transmission model of teaching.
The gap is not primarily a prompting problem. The system prompt already told the LLM it could redirect, address the class, and use various pedagogical moves. It did redirect — but without discriminative judgment about when. The structural absence is access to physical, temporal, social, and relational information.
LLM responses may be more useful as contrast objects than simulations. Where they add value — mathematical accuracy, procedural clarity — they function as idealised instructional texts, not classroom talk. Comparing LLM output against human discourse reveals how much of real teaching is not about delivering content.
